## Why Business Prompts Are Different

Consumer prompts focus on simple tasks: write an email, summarize this article, explain this concept. Business prompts require a fundamentally different approach because business decisions involve nuance, tradeoffs, and context that generic prompts cannot capture.

Effective business prompts share three characteristics:

**Context Density**: They provide enough background for the AI to understand your specific situation, not just the general category of problem you face.

**Structured Output**: They specify exactly how you want the response formatted, making it easier to act on the output.

**Decision Framework**: They ask for analysis and recommendations rather than just information, turning AI into an advisor rather than a search engine.

The prompts in this guide follow this pattern consistently. You will notice that each one asks you to fill in specific details about your business. This is not optional. The more context you provide, the more useful the output becomes.

## Strategy Prompts for Smarter Decisions

Strategic thinking is where AI adds the most leverage. A good strategy prompt can compress weeks of analysis into hours, helping you see your business from angles you might miss on your own.

### Prompt 1: SWOT Analysis Deep Dive

Most SWOT analyses are surface-level lists that do not lead to action. This prompt forces specificity and prioritization.

```
Conduct a thorough SWOT analysis for my business.

Business: [describe your business in 2-3 sentences]
Industry: [your industry]
Current stage: [startup/growth/mature]
Annual revenue: [approximate range]
Primary competitors: [list 2-3 main competitors]
Goals for next 12 months: [what you want to achieve]

For each quadrant, provide:
- 5 specific, actionable items (not generic observations)
- Priority ranking (high/medium/low impact)
- One recommended action for each item

STRENGTHS: Focus on what creates defensible advantage
WEAKNESSES: Focus on what could cause failure if not addressed
OPPORTUNITIES: Focus on trends and gaps you could exploit
THREATS: Focus on specific risks with likelihood assessment

End with your top 3 strategic priorities based on this analysis.
```

### Prompt 2: Competitive Positioning Analyzer

Understanding your competitive position is essential for pricing, marketing, and product decisions. This prompt maps your position relative to alternatives.

```
Analyze my competitive position in the market.

My business: [what you sell and to whom]
My primary competitors:
1. [Competitor 1]: [brief description]
2. [Competitor 2]: [brief description]
3. [Competitor 3]: [brief description]

My current positioning: [how you describe yourself]
My price point: [relative to competitors]
My unique strengths: [what you do better]

Analyze:
1. Create a positioning map with relevant axes
2. Identify where each competitor is positioned
3. Find underserved positions in the market
4. Evaluate my current position's defensibility
5. Recommend positioning adjustments with rationale
6. Suggest messaging that reinforces optimal position
```

### Prompt 3: Revenue Diversification Planner

Single revenue streams create fragile businesses. This prompt helps you identify realistic expansion opportunities.

```
Help me diversify my revenue streams.

Current business: [what you do now]
Current revenue sources:
- [Source 1]: [% of revenue]
- [Source 2]: [% of revenue]
Current annual revenue: [range]
Available resources: [capital, team capacity, skills]
Risk tolerance: [conservative/moderate/aggressive]

Suggest 5-7 new revenue streams that:
- Build on existing capabilities and customers
- Can be tested with minimal investment
- Have potential to reach 10%+ of current revenue
- Do not cannibalize existing offerings

For each suggestion:
- What it is and how it works
- Startup cost and time estimate
- Revenue potential (realistic range)
- Risk level with key assumptions
- First three steps to validate it
```

### Prompt 4: Business Model Canvas Generator

The Business Model Canvas forces clarity about how all pieces of your business connect. This prompt creates a comprehensive canvas with strategic insights.

```
Create a complete Business Model Canvas for my business.

Business description: [what you do]
Stage: [idea/startup/growth/mature]
Target customer: [who you serve]
Current challenges: [what problems you face]

Generate all 9 sections with 4-5 specific points each:
1. Customer Segments (be specific about demographics, psychographics)
2. Value Propositions (benefits, not features)
3. Channels (how customers find and buy from you)
4. Customer Relationships (how you maintain relationships)
5. Revenue Streams (how you make money)
6. Key Resources (what you need to deliver)
7. Key Activities (what you must do well)
8. Key Partnerships (who you depend on)
9. Cost Structure (major cost categories)

Then identify:
- The 3 riskiest assumptions in this model
- How to test each assumption cheaply
- The single biggest bottleneck to growth
```

## Marketing Prompts for Growth

Marketing prompts help you understand customers, craft messages, and build campaigns that convert. These prompts focus on strategy rather than content creation.

### Prompt 5: Customer Avatar Deep Dive

Understanding your customer at a granular level transforms every marketing decision. This prompt builds detailed personas based on real patterns.

```
Create a detailed customer avatar for my business.

Product/service: [what you sell]
Price point: [cost to customer]
Problem solved: [core pain point addressed]
Current customers (if any): [who buys now]

Build a complete profile including:

Demographics:
- Age range, income level, location, occupation
- Family situation, education level

Psychographics:
- Values and beliefs that affect purchase decisions
- Interests and hobbies
- Media consumption habits
- Aspirations and life goals

Pain Points:
- 5 specific frustrations related to what you solve
- What they have tried before
- Why previous solutions failed

Buying Behavior:
- Where they research solutions
- Who influences their decisions
- Objections they will have
- What triggers purchase urgency

Create 2-3 distinct avatars if your market has clear segments.
```

### Prompt 6: Marketing Channel Prioritizer

Most businesses spread marketing budget across too many channels. This prompt helps you focus on what will actually work.

```
Help me prioritize marketing channels for my business.

Business: [what you sell]
Target customer: [who you serve]
Price point: [customer acquisition value]
Current channels: [what you are doing now]
Monthly marketing budget: [available spend]
Team capacity: [who can execute marketing]

Analyze these channels for my specific situation:
1. SEO/Content marketing
2. Paid search (Google Ads)
3. Paid social (Facebook, Instagram, LinkedIn, TikTok)
4. Email marketing
5. Social media organic
6. Partnerships/affiliates
7. PR/Media
8. Events/networking
9. Referral programs
10. Cold outreach

For each channel, rate:
- Fit for my business (1-10 with explanation)
- Competition level
- Cost to test effectively
- Time to see results
- Scalability if it works

Recommend top 3 channels to focus on with 90-day action plan for each.
```

### Prompt 7: Value Proposition Refiner

A compelling value proposition is the foundation of all marketing. This prompt creates multiple versions you can test.

```
Help me sharpen my value proposition.

What I sell: [product/service description]
Who I serve: [target customer]
Problem I solve: [core pain point]
How I solve it: [your approach]
Alternatives available: [what else they could use]
My key differentiator: [what makes me different]

Create these versions of my value proposition:

1. One sentence (elevator pitch - under 15 words)
2. Three sentences (website hero section)
3. Paragraph format (about page or landing page)
4. Bullet point format (sales page or pitch deck)
5. Question format (ad copy angle)
6. Before/After format (transformation focus)

Each version should:
- Lead with the benefit, not the feature
- Address a specific pain point
- Differentiate from alternatives
- Include proof element if applicable

Then suggest A/B test variations for the top 2 versions.
```

### Prompt 8: Email Sequence Architecture

Email remains the highest-ROI marketing channel. This prompt creates sequences that nurture and convert.

```
Design an email marketing sequence for my business.

Business: [what you sell]
Email list: [type of subscribers - leads, customers, etc.]
Primary goal: [what you want email to accomplish]
Average deal size: [revenue per customer]

Create a 6-email welcome/nurture sequence:

For each email specify:
- Subject line (with backup option)
- Preview text
- Core message/theme
- Call to action
- Optimal send timing (days after signup)
- Length recommendation

Sequence arc:
Email 1: Welcome and immediate value delivery
Email 2: Share origin story and build trust
Email 3: Address common problems/objections
Email 4: Introduce solution with case study
Email 5: Social proof and overcome objections
Email 6: Direct offer with urgency

Include re-engagement sequence for subscribers who do not open.
```

## Operations Prompts for Efficiency

Operations prompts help you systemize your business, eliminate bottlenecks, and free up time for higher-value activities.

### Prompt 9: Process Optimization Audit

Every business has processes that waste time. This prompt identifies where to automate, delegate, or eliminate.

```
Audit and optimize this business process.

Process name: [what process]
Purpose: [why this process exists]
Current steps:
1. [Step 1]
2. [Step 2]
3. [Step 3]
[continue listing all steps]

Frequency: [how often this happens]
Time per occurrence: [how long it takes]
People involved: [who does what]
Current pain points: [what is frustrating]
Error rate: [how often mistakes happen]

Analyze and recommend:
1. Steps to eliminate (provide no value)
2. Steps to automate (repetitive, rule-based)
3. Steps to combine (unnecessary handoffs)
4. Steps to simplify (overcomplicated)
5. New tools to consider
6. Optimal sequence/order
7. Quality checkpoints to add
8. Estimated time savings

Create an improved SOP with clear ownership and timelines.
```

### Prompt 10: Automation Opportunity Finder

Automation is the key to scaling without proportionally increasing costs. This prompt identifies your best automation opportunities.

```
Find automation opportunities in my business.

Business type: [what you do]
Team size: [number of people]
Current tools: [software you already use]
Budget for automation: [monthly spend available]
Biggest time sinks: [where you waste the most time]

For each of these areas, identify automation opportunities:
1. Lead generation and capture
2. Sales follow-up and nurturing
3. Customer onboarding
4. Invoicing and payments
5. Reporting and analytics
6. Customer support
7. Marketing execution
8. Internal communication
9. Data entry and management
10. Scheduling and coordination

For each opportunity:
- What specifically to automate
- Recommended tool(s)
- Setup complexity (1-10)
- Monthly cost estimate
- Hours saved per week
- ROI timeline (when it pays for itself)

Prioritize opportunities by impact vs effort.
```

### Prompt 11: Meeting Efficiency Redesign

Most businesses waste hours in unnecessary or poorly-run meetings. This prompt redesigns your meeting culture.

```
Audit and optimize my meeting schedule.

Current recurring meetings:
[List each meeting with: name, purpose, attendees, length, frequency]

For each meeting, analyze:
- Should this meeting exist at all?
- Who actually needs to attend?
- What is the right length?
- What is the right frequency?
- Could this be asynchronous instead?

Then provide:
1. Recommended meeting structure (which to keep, modify, eliminate)
2. Agenda templates for essential meetings
3. Async alternatives (Slack, Loom, written updates)
4. Meeting-free time blocks recommendation
5. Estimated hours saved per week
6. Rules for when new meetings should be created
```

### Prompt 12: Standard Operating Procedure Creator

SOPs enable delegation and consistency. This prompt creates comprehensive procedures for any process.

```
Create a detailed Standard Operating Procedure.

Process: [what task or process]
Purpose: [why this is done]
Frequency: [how often]
Person responsible: [role, not specific person]
Tools required: [software, equipment]
Dependencies: [what must happen before this]

Create an SOP with:
1. Title and version number
2. Purpose and scope
3. Prerequisites (what is needed before starting)
4. Step-by-step instructions (numbered, unambiguous)
5. Decision points (if X, do Y; if Z, do W)
6. Quality checkpoints (how to verify correct completion)
7. Common mistakes and how to avoid them
8. Troubleshooting section (when things go wrong)
9. Completion checklist
10. Escalation procedures
11. Time estimates for each section

Format for easy reference during execution.
```

## Hiring Prompts for Building Your Team

Hiring mistakes are expensive. These prompts help you define roles, attract candidates, and evaluate effectively.

### Prompt 13: Job Description Generator

A great job description attracts qualified candidates and repels poor fits. This prompt creates descriptions that sell the opportunity while being honest about requirements.

```
Create a compelling job description.

Role title: [position name]
Reports to: [manager role]
Team size: [team they join]
Company stage: [startup/growth/enterprise]
Industry: [your industry]
Location: [remote/hybrid/onsite + location]
Salary range: [compensation]

Core responsibilities: [what they will do]
Must-have skills: [non-negotiable requirements]
Nice-to-have skills: [preferred but not required]
Company culture: [how you would describe it]
Growth opportunity: [career path]
Unique perks: [what makes you attractive]

Create a job description that:
1. Opens with a hook that attracts A-players
2. Describes the impact of this role (not just tasks)
3. Lists requirements in order of importance
4. Sells the company and opportunity
5. Includes deal-breakers clearly
6. Ends with clear application instructions

Make it scannable and avoid corporate jargon.
```

### Prompt 14: Interview Question Framework

Structured interviews predict job performance better than unstructured conversations. This prompt creates competency-based questions.

```
Create an interview framework for hiring.

Role: [position]
Key competencies needed:
1. [Competency 1]
2. [Competency 2]
3. [Competency 3]
4. [Competency 4]
5. [Competency 5]

Critical experiences: [what they must have done before]
Red flags: [warning signs to watch for]
Team culture: [how your team works]

Create:

For each competency, provide:
- 2 behavioral interview questions (past behavior)
- 2 situational questions (hypothetical scenarios)
- What a strong answer looks like
- What a weak answer looks like
- Follow-up probes to dig deeper

Include:
- Culture fit questions
- Motivation and career goal questions
- Questions they should ask you (and what good questions indicate)
- Scorecard template for evaluation

Total interview should be 45-60 minutes.
```

### Prompt 15: Candidate Evaluation Rubric

Consistent evaluation prevents bias and improves hiring decisions. This prompt creates objective scoring criteria.

```
Create a candidate evaluation rubric.

Role: [position]
Interview stages: [list your interview process]
Hiring criteria:
1. [Criterion 1]: [why it matters]
2. [Criterion 2]: [why it matters]
3. [Criterion 3]: [why it matters]
4. [Criterion 4]: [why it matters]
5. [Criterion 5]: [why it matters]

Non-negotiables: [absolute requirements]
Dealbreakers: [automatic disqualifiers]

Create a scoring rubric with:

For each criterion:
- Score 1: Does not meet requirements (describe what this looks like)
- Score 2: Partially meets requirements (describe)
- Score 3: Meets requirements (describe)
- Score 4: Exceeds requirements (describe)
- Score 5: Exceptional (describe)

Include:
- Weighting for each criterion (% of total)
- Minimum acceptable total score
- How to handle disagreements between interviewers
- Reference check questions for top candidates
- Decision-making framework for final selection
```

## How to Customize These Prompts

These prompts are templates, not scripts. Here is how to make them work for your specific situation.

### Add Context Aggressively

The more specific you are, the better the output. Instead of saying "small business," say "7-person B2B SaaS company selling project management software to marketing agencies, $800K ARR, based in Austin." Context allows AI to tailor recommendations to your reality.

### Include Constraints

Every business has constraints: budget limits, team capacity, technical capabilities, market restrictions. State these explicitly. If you cannot spend more than $2,000/month on tools, say so. If you have no developers, say so. Constraints shape realistic recommendations.

### Request Multiple Options

Ask for multiple alternatives rather than a single recommendation. "Give me three options with tradeoffs for each" produces more useful output than "what should I do." You can evaluate options based on factors the AI does not know about.

### Iterate Based on Output

Your first prompt rarely produces perfect output. Use follow-up prompts to refine: "Make option 2 more specific," "What are the risks of this approach," "How would this work with a smaller budget." Think of it as a conversation, not a single query.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

### Mistake 1: Being Too Vague

"Help me with my marketing" tells AI nothing. "Help me create a content marketing strategy to generate leads for my accounting software for small restaurants" gives AI something to work with.

### Mistake 2: Accepting First Drafts

AI output is a starting point, not a finished product. Always review critically. Ask yourself: Does this actually apply to my situation? What context is the AI missing? What would I change?

### Mistake 3: Skipping the Context

Filling in the brackets feels tedious, but it is what makes these prompts work. Taking five minutes to provide detailed context saves hours of refining generic output.

### Mistake 4: Not Verifying Claims

AI can generate plausible-sounding information that is incorrect. Verify specific claims, statistics, and recommendations before acting on them. Use AI for structure and ideas, not as your only source of truth.

### Mistake 5: Using AI for Everything

Some decisions require human judgment, relationships, and intuition that AI cannot replicate. Use AI to prepare for important conversations and decisions, but do not let it replace critical thinking.
